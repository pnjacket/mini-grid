# Concern 11.13 — Performance & Scalability

> **One-line:** How fast, under how much load, and how it grows — the measurable targets, the load it must hold, and the strategy for scaling to meet them.

## Purpose & Scope

Active when there are real throughput/latency/scale requirements. Owns performance **targets**, **load profiles**, and the **scaling strategy + capacity model**. Architecture provides enabling patterns; Operations implements the scaling mechanism; Observability measures the targets.

## Sub-aspects (the scope vocabulary)

| Sub-aspect | key | Default (when in scope) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| `PERF-###` targets | `perf-targets` | in | Metric + threshold + load condition + measurement boundary; algorithm+window if a rate limit; sample population/window/re-run rule if a percentile |
| Load profiles | `load-profiles` | in | Expected / peak / growth. For a **real-time/client** product, the load condition is a **workload script** (`SEQ-###`) — see the bar |
| Scaling strategy + capacity model | `scaling-strategy-capacity-model` | in | Horizontal/vertical, capacity per unit |
| Bottleneck analysis | `bottleneck-analysis` | in | Known limits and hotspots |
| Resource budgets | `resource-budgets` | optional | CPU/memory/cost envelopes |

## Owned Contracts (referenced by stable ID)

1. **Performance targets** — `PERF-###`: metric + threshold + load condition + **measurement boundary** (end-to-end vs server-side — or, for client/real-time products, main-thread vs worker-seam vs compositor); where the target is a rate limit, also its **algorithm + window** (e.g. token-bucket, 1s window); where the target is a **percentile**, also its **sample population, window, and re-run rule** (co-owned with Quality 11.5's flake/re-run policy). **Referenced by** Product & Requirements (the NFR target it summarizes), Observability (`SLO-###` measure them), Quality & Testing (perf tests assert them).
2. **Load profiles** — expected/peak/growth; for a frame-budget/real-time product, the **workload script** (`SEQ-###`) that *is* each target's load condition. **Referenced by** Quality (perf-test basis).
3. **Scaling strategy + capacity model.**
4. **Measurement hooks (conditional)** — when Observability (11.11) is out of scope, Performance **owns the measurement-hooks contract**: the marks/counters/probes the product must expose for its `PERF-###` targets to be measured at all (mirroring the Ops-override pattern — the need doesn't vanish with the concern that usually owns it).

## Consumed (referenced, not owned)

- `CAP-###` (which capabilities carry targets); `COMPONENT-###` (what scales); `PATTERN-###` (Architecture's enabling patterns).

## Contract-grade bar

`PERF-###` targets + load profiles + scaling strategy + capacity model + bottleneck analysis.

- **Each target states its measurement boundary.** A threshold is ambiguous without where it's measured — **end-to-end** (client-observed, including network) vs **server-side** (handler latency); for client/real-time products the enumeration extends to **main-thread**, **worker-seam**, and **compositor**. Without it, the doc and the perf test can disagree on what passed.
- **A rate-limit target states its algorithm + window, not just the number.** "5/s, burst 10" doesn't tell an implementer token-bucket vs fixed-window vs sliding-window; the choice changes the exact edge a test asserts. Name the algorithm and its window alongside the threshold.
- **A percentile target states its sample population, window, and re-run rule** (co-owned with Quality 11.5). "p95 ≥ 30 fps" is untestable until the row says what the samples are, over which window, and what a re-run means. The row's **method** details are part of the contract, not harness discretion: how the window is partitioned **including the trailing partial bucket's disposition** (count it, or exclude whole buckets only), and the **sample source** for an "N runs" aggregate (separate harness invocations vs N fresh contexts within one execution).
- **A sequence-shaped load condition states its feasibility assumptions — or delegates route planning explicitly.** For real-time/client products, load collapses into each target via a **workload script** (`SEQ-###`): a contracted input sequence (move here, edit there, hold this key) standing where "requests/second" would. Such a script presumes the product's content makes it executable (terrain can wall a fixed walk line; a required edit position may need steering); the row either states those assumptions or delegates planning to the sanctioned expectation-precomputation pattern (pure product modules in the test process — 11.5).
- **Ownership boundary with Operations:** Performance owns the capacity **envelope** (the `resource-budgets` CPU/memory/cost targets and the capacity model); **Operations owns its realization** (the deployment's actual requests/limits and scaling mechanism).

## Worked Example (a planning SaaS)

At *Contract-grade*:
- `PERF-VIEW-OPEN` — p95 < 500ms (**server-side**, handler latency) to open a grid view at 10k rows; this is the basis for the row-count "performance degradation warning" in `16-views.md`.
- `PERF-CALC-RUN` — calculation chain throughput target.
- `PERF-RATE-LIMITS` — 5 req/s per user, burst 10, via **token-bucket with a 1s refill window** (not just the "5/s" number); Quality's perf test asserts the bucket's exact edge.
- **Load profile:** tenants × models × concurrent contributors at peak.
- **Scaling:** per-service horizontal scaling; PostgreSQL as the known bottleneck. Observability's `SLO-VIEW-OPEN` measures `PERF-VIEW-OPEN`.

## Design Decisions

| # | Decision | Rationale |
|---|----------|-----------|
| 1 | `PERF-###` targets carry a load condition. | A latency number without its load is unfalsifiable; pairing them makes targets testable and SLO-able. |
| 2 | Performance owns strategy + capacity; Architecture enables, Ops implements. | Three-way split keeps the "how fast / how much" reasoning here, the structural patterns in Architecture, and the runtime mechanism in Ops. |
| 3 | Owns load profiles, referenced by Quality. | One source for "how much load we expect" so perf tests and capacity planning agree. |
| 4 | Contract-grade requires bottleneck analysis. | Naming the limiting resource is what turns a scaling *aspiration* into a scaling *plan*. |
| 5 | A target states its measurement boundary; a rate limit states its algorithm + window. | A threshold number alone is under-specified — end-to-end vs server-side, and token-bucket vs fixed-window, change what a test asserts, so the doc and the test can silently disagree. |
| 6 | Performance owns the capacity envelope; Operations owns its realization. | `resource-budgets` is the planning target (the envelope) here; the deployment's actual requests/limits and scaling mechanism are Ops. Stating the boundary stops the two concerns from each owning half of "how much resource." |
| 7 | The **real-time/client profile** is named: workload-script load conditions (`SEQ-###`) and a boundary enumeration extended with main-thread / worker-seam / compositor. | The vocabulary was request/response-shaped; a frame-budget product (a client-only browser game) has no requests to count — its load *is* a contracted input sequence, and its boundaries are where frame time is spent. Without the profile, every such product re-derives the mapping. |
| 8 | A percentile target carries its sample population, window, re-run rule, and method details (partial-bucket disposition, sample source). | A p95 frame-rate target proved unmeasurable as written: the trailing partial window had no stated disposition and "median of 3 runs" didn't say where the 3 samples come from. Each unstated detail is a place the doc and the evidence can disagree (failure-mode #24). |
| 9 | A sequence-shaped load condition states feasibility assumptions or delegates route planning. | A scripted "walk a fixed compass line" load condition silently presumed walkable terrain; the first evidence run got zero qualifying samples. The script is a contract about the product's content as much as about input, so the assumption must be stated or the planning delegated. |
| 10 | When 11.11 is out of scope, Performance owns the measurement-hooks contract. | Targets are only contracts if something can measure them; with Observability legitimately out, the marks/counters the product must expose had no named home and were housed here by derivation. Naming the override makes it mechanical (same shape as the Ops trigger override). |
